                              Chapter 8
           Social, Legal, Ethical, Sustainability and Safety Aspects

In this chapter, the ATSF model for mineral commodity demand forecasting is
examined from the perspectives of data ethics, societal responsibility, legal
compliance, and long-term sustainability. The analysis covers data privacy
standards, sources of algorithmic bias inherent to the evaluated datasets,
the potential for misuse given the model's procurement risk outputs, alignment
with international regulatory frameworks for mineral resources and AI systems,
and the project's contribution to global sustainability through its alignment
with United Nations Sustainable Development Goals. These considerations are
integral to the responsible engineering of any AI system deployed in critical
supply-chain decision contexts.

________________________________________________________________________________
8.1 PRIVACY CONSIDERATIONS
________________________________________________________________________________

The ATSF model was developed with strict adherence to data privacy and ethical
research standards throughout all phases of the project. No private procurement
data, sensitive enterprise records, commercially restricted trade information,
or personally identifiable information (PII) were used at any stage of training,
validation, or evaluation. Both datasets utilised — the SupplyGraph benchmark [19]
and the USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries 2024 [20] — are publicly available
for academic research purposes and are published by recognised institutional
sources: the AAAI Graphs and Complex Structures workshop and the United States
Geological Survey respectively.

8.1.1 PROOF-OF-CONCEPT DISCLOSURE FOR RISK SCORING:

Layer 5, the rule-based risk scoring interface implemented in
risk_decision_layer.py, produces procurement risk assessments based on point
forecasts, GAT attention weights, and configurable thresholds sourced from
experiments/config.yaml. It is explicitly and prominently disclosed that the
lead-time proxy values used by this layer are synthetic: SupplyGraph uses
group-level constants sampled from the range [3, 30] days; USGS uses a fixed
value of 1.0. These proxies do not represent real logistics data from any
supply-chain operator. The risk assessment outputs — budget_risk,
leadtime_risk, dependency_risk, overall_risk_score, and action flags — are
representative demonstrations of the decision-support architecture and must not
be deployed in real-world operational procurement scenarios without validation
against actual transport logs and domain-expert review. This layer is formally
classified as a proof-of-concept and is documented as Limitation 4 of the
model [Section 3.5].

8.1.2 DATA USE SCOPE:

The node features constructed by graph_builder.py — recent demand average,
price-level proxy, production capacity utilisation, and normalised lead time —
are all derived from aggregated commodity-level statistics sourced from the
public datasets. No individual enterprise production records, no company-level
financial data, and no trade partner identities are represented in the node
feature matrices. Edge confidence weights in all_edges.csv are derived from
published materials science and supply-chain literature, not from proprietary
industrial relationship databases.

8.2 ALGORITHMIC BIAS AND FAIRNESS
________________________________________________________________________________

Two distinct sources of algorithmic bias have been identified and documented
within the evaluated datasets, consistent with the transparency requirements
of responsible AI development.

8.2.1 GEOGRAPHIC BIAS IN SUPPLYGRAPH:

The SupplyGraph test partition is predominantly populated by products belonging
to the European Economic Area (EEA) product group. This geographic
concentration arises from the chronological 70/15/15 split applied to the
222-day trading window: since the test set comprises the final 15% of the
time series, and the EEA product group has the highest representation in the
latter portion of the dataset, test-set evaluation is necessarily biased
toward late-period EEA demand behaviour [Limitation 3]. Users applying the
trained SupplyGraph model to product groups with different seasonal patterns or
market structures should interpret the reported WAPE of 55.07%+-1.94% with
appropriate caution, as generalisation to non-EEA product groups has not been
empirically validated.

8.2.2 LOW-DATA REGIME BIAS IN USGS:

The USGS dataset operates in a severe low-data regime: each of the 127 mineral
commodity nodes has at most five annual observations, of which only 3-4 are
used for training under the rolling-window cross-validation scheme [Limitation 1].
While multi-seed replication across five independent seeds mitigates the risk
of seed-specific overfitting — confirmed by the convergence report in Section
6.8 — binary conclusions regarding individual mineral commodity demand
trajectories should be interpreted with caution. The small sample size (n=5
seeds) also limits the achievable Wilcoxon signed-rank p-value to a minimum
of p=0.0625 as a mathematical floor [Limitation 2], precluding conventional
statistical significance thresholds.

8.2.3 GENERALISATION LIMITATIONS:

The model's forecasting performance has been validated on the two specific
datasets described in Section 5.4. Its fairness and accuracy in other industrial
domains — including metals not represented in the USGS commodity list,
supply chains at sub-annual temporal resolution, or geographies not covered
by USGS reporting — remain to be empirically tested [Limitation 7]. Any
deployment of the ATSF model beyond the two evaluated datasets must be
accompanied by appropriate domain-specific validation.

8.3 POTENTIAL FOR MISUSE
________________________________________________________________________________

8.3.1 MISINTERPRETATION OF THE RISK SCORING LAYER:

The most significant misuse risk associated with the ATSF system is the
deployment of the Layer 5 risk scoring outputs in live operational procurement
decisions without understanding their proof-of-concept status. The risk
assessment thresholds — budget_threshold=100,000, lead_time_safety_factor=1.5,
criticality_threshold=0.7 — are configurable defaults set in experiments/
config.yaml, and the thresholds governing action flag generation were designed
for demonstration purposes, not calibrated against historical procurement
outcomes. Using these outputs to trigger real supplier expediting, backup
supplier activation, or financial budget reallocation without domain-expert
validation would constitute a misuse of the system.

8.3.2 MISINTERPRETATION OF GEOPOLITICAL EDGE LABELS:

The USGS supply-chain graph contains a geopolitical_supply_risk edge type
(ID 8), which connects mineral commodity pairs that share documented
geopolitical supply concentration risk as reported in the USGS Mineral
Commodity Summaries 2024 [20]. It must be clearly understood that the ATSF
model is a demand-forecasting system, not a geopolitical prediction or risk
rating system. The geopolitical_supply_risk edge label encodes an existing
documented structural relationship between commodities — it reflects structural
relatedness in supply concentration, not a capability to forecast geopolitical
events. Users must not interpret the edge ablation result for
geopolitical_supply_risk (Delta RMSE = -0.55% when removed, classifying it as
causally important) as a endorsement of any geopolitical risk rating or
country-specific supply chain assessment.

8.3.3 ADVERSARIAL QUERY RISKS IN THE AGENTIC PLATFORM:

The Streamlit-based procurement agent (Tab 3) routes user natural-language
queries to the meta/llama-3.1-70b-instruct model via the NVIDIA NIM API.
Although the system prompt explicitly instructs the model to ground its outputs
in the verified ATSF structural trace — and the native subgraph extraction step
prevents hallucinated commodity names from entering the pipeline — adversarial
or ambiguous user inputs that bypass the entity extraction step could produce
procurement advice detached from the empirical model outputs. Deployment in
high-stakes procurement environments requires additional input sanitisation and
human review of all LLM-generated procurement narratives before operational use.

8.4 REGULATORY COMPLIANCE AND LEGAL FRAMEWORKS
________________________________________________________________________________

8.4.1 DATA SOURCE COMPLIANCE:

All USGS data is sourced from the official Mineral Commodity Summaries 2024
publication of the United States Geological Survey [20], a publicly available
government publication issued under open-access terms for research and
educational use. The SupplyGraph data is sourced from the AAAI 2024 Graphs and
More Complex Structures workshop benchmark dataset [19], which is published
under academic research licence terms. No proprietary, classified, restricted-
access, or regulated industrial data was used at any stage of the project.
The project therefore operates in full compliance with open-research regulations
and Presidency University ethics policies governing academic data use.

8.4.2 ALIGNMENT WITH INTERNATIONAL MINERAL REPORTING STANDARDS:

The project's framing of mineral commodity demand — particularly its focus on
critical minerals such as lithium, cobalt, and copper — is aligned with the
International Energy Agency's Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2024 [17], which
provides the authoritative global framework for understanding the strategic
importance of critical mineral supply security in the context of the clean energy
transition. The USGS dataset, which serves as the primary source of annual
production, import, and export statistics for 127 mineral commodities, is the
official reference publication used by the IEA in its own supply security
assessments, ensuring that the project's data sources are consistent with
international mineral reporting standards.

8.4.3 AI SYSTEM TRANSPARENCY REQUIREMENTS:

In alignment with emerging AI transparency frameworks, this project provides
the following disclosures: (1) the complete model architecture, training
configuration, and all verified performance metrics are documented in this
report; (2) all seven known limitations of the model are explicitly stated
in Section 9.2; (3) the risk scoring layer is clearly labelled as a
proof-of-concept and not operationally validated; (4) the statistical
limitations of the five-seed evaluation — including the minimum achievable
Wilcoxon p-value of 0.0625 — are transparently reported rather than obscured;
and (5) the synthetic nature of the lead-time proxy values used in Layer 5
is documented at every point of presentation.

8.5 SUSTAINABILITY AND SDG IMPACT
________________________________________________________________________________

As introduced in Section 1.6, the ATSF model directly supports three of the
United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, contributing to the broader
international sustainability agenda in the context of critical mineral supply
chains and the global clean energy transition [17].

8.5.1 SDG 9: INDUSTRY, INNOVATION AND INFRASTRUCTURE:

SDG 9 calls for the building of resilient infrastructure, the promotion of
inclusive and sustainable industrialisation, and the fostering of innovation.
The ATSF model supports SDG 9 by advancing the state of the art in multi-modal
AI for critical supply chain management. By replacing fixed, inflexible fusion
assumptions with a learned adaptive mechanism, the project demonstrates how
AI innovation can produce more reliable industrial intelligence for procurement
planners. The interactive Streamlit agentic platform further advances this goal
by making complex graph neural network outputs accessible to non-technical
procurement stakeholders through natural-language query interfaces, reducing
the barrier to technology adoption in industrial supply chain contexts.

8.5.2 SDG 12: RESPONSIBLE CONSUMPTION AND PRODUCTION:

SDG 12 calls for ensuring sustainable consumption and production patterns.
Accurate demand forecasting is a prerequisite for responsible production, as
it reduces the material waste associated with over-procurement, excess inventory,
and systemic forecasting errors in mineral supply chains. By achieving a USGS
WAPE of 7.01%+-0.83% — below the XGBoost baseline of 8.07% — the ATSF model
demonstrates a quantifiably more precise forecast signal for annual mineral
demand, which translates directly to more accurate sourcing and stockpile
planning decisions and a corresponding reduction in procurement inefficiency
and wasted resource acquisition.

8.5.3 SDG 13: CLIMATE ACTION:

SDG 13 calls for urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts. The
global transition to clean energy technologies — electric vehicles, renewable
energy storage, and grid infrastructure — depends critically on the secure and
predictable supply of a set of key mineral commodities including lithium,
cobalt, nickel, and copper [17]. Accurate demand forecasting for these
commodities supports long-term capital planning for mining and refining
investment, reduces supply disruptions that delay clean energy deployments, and
enables procurement strategies that account for geopolitical supply risks.
The IEA's Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2024 [17] explicitly identifies
improved forecasting methodology as a key gap in supporting the energy
transition, providing direct institutional context for the societal relevance
of this project.
