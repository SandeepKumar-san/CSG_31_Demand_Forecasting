

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
                                     ABSTRACT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Forecasting demand for mineral commodities and industrial supply chains involves 
both temporal sequence modelling and structural graph reasoning. However, 
existing hybrid approaches often fail because they rely on a fixed, 
pre-specified fusion weight for each modality, assuming a constant balance 
that does not hold across datasets with different characteristics. In this 
project, we propose the Adaptive Temporal-Structural Fusion (ATSF) model, 
which learns per-instance scalar fusion weights (alpha) conditioned on 
material type and forecast horizon.

The ATSF model integrates a Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) branch to capture 
long-range temporal dependencies and a multi-relational Graph Attention 
Network (GAT) branch to encode inter-commodity relationships. A learned 
adaptive fusion layer dynamically determines the optimal balance between these 
modalities on a per-instance basis, replacing traditional fixed-weight blending 
and entropy regularisation with a novel one-sided boundary regularisation 
mechanism.

Our empirical evaluation on two structurally distinct datasets—daily SupplyGraph 
and annual USGS mineral data—demonstrates the model's superior performance over 
all fixed-weight neural alternatives. On the SupplyGraph dataset, ATSF 
achieved a WAPE of 55.07%+-1.94%, representing a 30.1% reduction over the best 
neural alternative. On the USGS dataset, the model attained a WAPE of 
7.01%+-0.83%, with a 95% confidence interval [5.99%, 8.03%] entirely below 
the deterministic XGBoost baseline of 8.07%. Furthermore, the learned alpha 
values revealed a 43-percentage-point structural contribution gap between the 
two datasets (alpha=0.919+-0.010 for SupplyGraph vs alpha=0.489+-0.197 for USGS), 
quantifying the dataset-dependent nature of modality importance. This project 
also implements a probabilistic uncertainty quantification layer providing 
calibrated P10-P90 prediction intervals and an illustrative rule-based 
risk-scoring interface for procurement planning.

                                  INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background

Mineral commodity demand forecasting is a critical component of global industrial 
stability, underpinning procurement planning, resource allocation, and 
geopolitical risk management. The demand for these commodities is governed by 
two distinct signals: temporal patterns, such as historical trends and 
seasonality, and structural patterns, which are inter-commodity dependencies 
encoded in complex supply-chain graphs. 

Existing methodologies often model these signals in isolation. Transformer-based 
models, such as the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) [1], are highly effective 
at capturing long-range temporal dynamics through self-attention mechanisms 
but generally ignore the structural relationships between different commodities. 
Conversely, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), such as Graph Attention Networks 
(GAT) [2], are designed to model structural dependencies across 
non-uniformly structured graphs [3] but often rely on fixed or simplistic 
temporal aggregation methods. 

While hybrid approaches [4] have been developed to combine both modalities, 
they typically impose a fixed fusion weight, implicitly assuming a constant 
balance between temporal and structural information signals across all 
scenarios. This assumption is often flawed; for instance, daily Fast-Moving 
Consumer Goods (FMCG) data are typically dominated by temporal dynamics, 
whereas annual mineral commodity data often exhibit balanced or ambiguous 
modality contributions due to sparse temporal observations and significantly 
stronger structural dependencies. This project addresses the limitation of 
fixed-fusion weights by proposing an adaptive mechanism that learns the 
optimal balance based on the data context.

1.2 Statistics of the Project

The performance and adaptive capabilities of the ATSF model are evaluated across 
two structurally distinct datasets that represent different forecasting 
challenges in the industrial and mineral sectors.

The SupplyGraph dataset [19] comprises daily operational demand data for 40 
products across 5 European Economic Area (EEA) product groups, covering 222 
trading days from 2022 to 2023. The associated supply-chain graph encodes 
4 typed edge relations: product group, sub-group, plant, and storage location. 
This dataset represents a scenario dominated by temporal dynamics with 
relatively shallow structural information.

The USGS Mineral Resources dataset [20] is derived from the United States 
Geological Survey's annual production and import statistics. It covers 127 
mineral commodities over a 5-year period from 2019 to 2023. Unlike the daily 
resolution of SupplyGraph, the USGS dataset operates at an annual resolution and 
features a much richer structural network with 15 typed edge relations, 
including technology clusters, alloy components, and geopolitical supply risks. 
This dataset represents a mixed-regime scenario where structural dependencies 
play a significantly more prominent role in demand forecasting.

1.3 Prior Existing Technologies

Prior research into commodity and industrial demand forecasting has primarily 
followed three distinct streams: temporal-only models, graph-only models, and 
fixed-fusion hybrid models.

Temporal-only models, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [5] and 
the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) [1], are designed to capture complex, 
long-range dependencies in historical demand data. While these models are highly 
effective at learning seasonality and cyclical trends, they are inherently 
limited by their inability to incorporate the structural relationships that 
exist between nodes in a supply-chain network. They treat each commodity as an 
independent entity, ignoring the dependencies derived from co-production or 
substitution.

Graph-only models, such as Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) [8], 
GraphSAGE [9], and Graph Attention Networks (GAT) [2], focus on modelling 
relational dependencies between entities. These approaches have been shown to 
reveal hidden links and dependencies in supply chains [12]. However, their 
performance in demand forecasting is often limited by a reliance on fixed or 
simplistic temporal aggregation methods, which fail to capture the dynamic 
nature of demand signals over time.

To address the limitations of these isolated approaches, several hybrid models 
have been proposed. For example, Graph WaveNet [13] and the multimodal 
spatial-temporal graph attention network (MST-GAT) [4] attempt to combine 
temporal and structural information. However, a significant limitation of these 
hybrid frameworks is their reliance on fixed fusion weights. These models 
implicitly assume that the balance between temporal and structural modalities is 
constant across all datasets and contexts. As demonstrated in this project, 
this assumption fails to generalise, especially when moving between daily FMCG 
data and annual mineral commodity data where the modality significance differs 
substantially.

1.4 Proposed Approach

To overcome the limitations of fixed-weight fusion, we propose the Adaptive 
Temporal-Structural Fusion (ATSF) model. The primary motivation for this 
approach is the recognition that the importance of temporal vs. structural 
information is non-constant and depends heavily on the dataset's resolution 
and relational complexity.

The proposed system architecture is a five-layer pipeline:
1.  **TFT Branch (Layer 1)**: Captures temporal dynamics using a Transformer 
    architecture with variable selection and self-attention.
2.  **GAT Branch (Layer 2)**: Encodes structural inter-commodity dependencies 
    through a multi-relational graph attention network.
3.  **Adaptive Fusion Layer (Layer 3)**: The core innovation, which learns a 
    per-instance scalar weight (alpha) to blend the two branches.
4.  **Demand Output Head (Layer 4)**: Maps the fused representation to 
    multi-horizon point and quantile forecasts.
5.  **Rule-based Risk Scoring Interface (Layer 5)**: A practitioner-configurable 
    decision-support module for procurement planning.

The ATSF model contains approximately 198,000 trainable parameters and is 
designed for applications in mineral commodity demand forecasting and industrial 
supply chain procurement. While the model is highly effective, it is subject to 
certain limitations, such as the low-data regime of annual observations and 
the reliance on static graph structures, which are detailed in Chapter 9.

1.5 Objectives

The primary objectives of this project are:
1.  To design and implement a per-instance scalar fusion mechanism (alpha) 
    conditioned on material type and forecast horizon for adaptive 
    temporal-structural integration.
2.  To empirically demonstrate that learned modality preference (alpha) varies 
    significantly across datasets with different structural characteristics.
3.  To evaluate ATSF performance against six baseline models (ARIMA, XGBoost, 
    TFT-only, GAT-only, Fixed Fusion) across two structurally distinct datasets 
    using WAPE, RMSE, R2, and SMAPE.
4.  To implement a probabilistic uncertainty quantification layer producing 
    calibrated P10-P90 prediction intervals.
5.  To analyse structural attention through edge ablation, quantifying the causal 
    contribution of each USGS edge type to forecasting performance.

                                   CHAPTER 2
                               LITERATURE REVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Lim et al. (2021) [1] introduced the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) as a novel 
deep learning architecture specifically designed for multi-horizon time series 
forecasting. The core contribution of this work is the integration of 
multi-head self-attention with recurrent networks to capture both long-term and 
short-term temporal dependencies across various scales. A key component of the 
TFT is the Variable Selection Network (VSN), which applies soft, learned 
feature gating to identify the most relevant static and dynamic covariates for 
each prediction step. This architecture not only provides multi-horizon quantile 
forecasts to quantify uncertainty but also offers interpretability through 
attention weights. While the TFT achieves superior performance on standard 
forecasting benchmarks, it treats multi-variate signals as isolated temporal 
features and does not explicitly incorporate the structural, relational 
dependencies that define supply-chain networks. Future work hinted at 
extending such interpretable mechanisms to spatial or graph-structured data.

Veličković et al. (2018) [2] proposed Graph Attention Networks (GAT), representing 
a significant advancement in graph neural networks by introducing masked 
self-attentional layers. Unlike previous graph convolutional networks that rely 
on fixed, pre-defined adjacency matrices, GAT allows for the dynamic and 
adaptive aggregation of information across node neighbourhoods. By assigning 
different weights to different nodes in a neighbourhood, the model can focus on 
more relevant neighbours without requiring computationally expensive matrix 
inversions. This attention mechanism is particularly well-suited for 
non-uniformly structured graphs, which are common in real-world supply chains. 
However, the original GAT formulation focuses on node-level classification 
and representation learning in static environments. For time-series applications, 
it must be augmented with temporal layers, as it lacks an inherent mechanism 
to model sequential dynamics or seasonal trends across long-term lookback windows.

Wu et al. (2021) [3] provided a comprehensive survey on the evolution and 
application of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), categorising them into graph 
convolutional, attentional, and generative networks. The survey highlights how 
GNNs have successfully bridged the gap between deep learning and relational 
data, enabling powerful reasoning over non-Euclidean spaces. A key finding is 
that GNNs are uniquely capable of capturing the underlying structural 
dependencies in complex systems like social networks and supply chains. 
Despite their success, the authors note that many GNN frameworks remain 
limited by their reliance on fixed temporal aggregation or "shallow" integration 
with time-series models. They suggest that future research should focus on 
scalable, adaptive architectures that can handle dynamic graphs where edge 
labels and intensities change over time. This survey serves as a foundational 
theoretical justification for incorporating multi-relational structural signals 
into forecasting pipelines.

Ding et al. (2023) [4] addressed the challenge of multi-modal data fusion with 
the introduction of MST-GAT, a multimodal spatial-temporal graph attention 
network. The model was specifically designed for time series anomaly detection 
by integrating temporal dynamics with spatial relationships within a unified 
graph framework. The authors demonstrate that combining these modalities leads 
to a more robust representation of system behaviour than using either modality 
in isolation. However, a major limitation identified in this approach is the 
use of fixed or non-explicit fusion mechanisms. By assuming a constant balance 
between temporal and spatial signals, the model may underperform when evaluated 
across datasets where one modality is significantly more dominant than the 
other. This project builds upon the MST-GAT concept but replaces the fixed 
balance with a context-conditioned adaptive fusion weight to resolve this 
generalisation issue.

Hochreiter and Schmidhuber (1997) [5] fundamentally changed the landscape of 
temporal modelling with the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network. By 
introducing the concept of "constant error carousels" and gating mechanisms 
(input, output, and forget gates), the LSTM successfully resolved the 
vanishing gradient problem that plagued traditional Recurrent Neural Networks 
(RNNs). This allowed the model to learn and maintain dependencies over 
extended time intervals, making it the industry standard for sequence modelling 
tasks such for decades. While newer architectures like Transformers have 
since emerged, LSTMs remain a robust component of many modern forecasting 
systems, including the TFT branch of our ATSF model. A noted limitation of the 
LSTM is its sequential processing nature, which can be computationally intensive 
for very long windows compared to parallelisable attention mechanisms. Furthermore, 
like all purely recurrent models, it possesses no native capability to reason 
about the complex, graph-based structural relationships between multiple 
interconnected time series.

Makridakis et al. (2020) [6] presented the results of the M4 competition, a large-scale evaluation of 61 forecasting methods across 100,000 time series. The study established major benchmarks in the field, demonstrating that hybrid models combining statistical and machine learning approaches consistently outperformed pure statistical or machine learning methods. A significant outcome for our project was the validation of Weighted Absolute Percentage Error (WAPE) and Symmetric Mean Absolute Percentage Error (SMAPE) as robust metrics for cross-series benchmarking, as they avoid the distortions associated with scale-dependent metrics. The findings also highlighted that while complexity can improve accuracy, model interpretability often suffers. This serves as a key motivation for our project's focus on the interpretable fusion weight alpha, which provides a readable diagnostic of dataset modality without compromising the performance gains of fixed hybrids.

Hosseinnia and Ghahnavieh (2023) [7] conducted a systematic literature review on the applications of deep learning in supply chain management (SCM). The authors categorized research into inventory management, demand forecasting, and logistics optimization, reporting that while deep learning models demonstrate exceptional theoretical performance in laboratories, there remains a persistent gap in their real-world deployment. This "research-practical gap" is often attributed to the lack of explainability and the difficulty of adapting complex models to the noisy, low-data environments typical of global supply chains. The study advocates for future research to focus on decision-aware forecasting systems that can quantify risk and provide actionable insights beyond simple point values. Our implementation of Layer 5 (Risk Scoring) and Layer 4 (Uncertainty Quantification) is a direct response to this call for more operationally relevant AI systems.

Kipf and Welling (2017) [8] introduced the Graph Convolutional Network (GCN), a foundational architecture for learning on graph-structured data. By proposing a first-order approximation of spectral graph convolutions, the GCN efficiently captures localized spatial features within node neighbourhoods. The model demonstrated remarkable performance in semi-supervised classification tasks by effectively propagating information across the graph surface. Despite its efficiency, a limitation of the GCN is its reliance on a fixed adjacency matrix and its isotropic nature—treating all neighbours as equally important in the aggregation process. Our project addresses this limitation by employing Graph Attention Networks (GAT) for our structural branch, which allows the model to learn which neighbours and connection types are most influential for demand signals, rather than assuming uniform importance.

Hamilton et al. (2017) [9] addressed the scalability issues of traditional graph neural networks with GraphSAGE, an inductive framework for learning low-dimensional representations of nodes. Unlike transductive models like GCN that require the entire graph during training, GraphSAGE learns an aggregator function that can be applied to previously unseen nodes. This makes it particularly effective for dynamic or large-scale graphs where the network topology may change. The authors explored various aggregation functions, such as mean and LSTM aggregators, to capture the richest possible local features. While GraphSAGE is powerful for representation learning, its performance in precision-forecasting tasks is sensitive to the choice of aggregation function and lookback window. This project leverages the inductive reasoning concepts of GraphSAGE but applies them via multidimensional attention gates to better handle the heterogeneity of supply-chain relations.

Jin et al. (2024) [10] published a comprehensive survey on the integration of graph neural networks and time series analysis. The survey explores current methodologies for forecasting, classification, and anomaly detection, emphasizing that the key challenge in spatio-temporal modelling is the "shallow" nature of many temporal-structural integrations. Many current models apply a fixed, non-learned balance between these two signals, assuming that their relative importance is constant across all prediction horizons. The authors emphasize that the next frontier in research resides in adaptive fusion mechanisms that can dynamically adjust the modality weight (e.g., temporal vs. RELATIONAL) based on the local data intensity. This survey provides the direct academic motivation for our "alpha" fusion layer, which converts this open research question into a concrete, learned component of the ATSF architecture.

Jiang and Luo (2022) [11] conducted a systematic survey on the application of graph neural networks in traffic forecasting. The authors highlight that traffic flow and demand forecasting share a common challenge: capturing the complex, non-linear spatio-temporal dependencies between different nodes in a network. The survey categorized methodologies into those using recurrent layers for temporal modelling and those using convolutional or attention mechanisms. A critical insight provided in the study is that static graphs often fail to represent the dynamic nature of road networks, where traffic patterns shift hourly. While supply chains often operate at significantly lower frequencies, the lesson from this traffic research is the importance of "adaptive" adjacency, where the model can learn and weight node relations based on the current context—a concept that our ATSF model adapts to the commodity domain.

Kosasih and Brintrup (2022) [12] explored a machine learning approach to predicting hidden links in supply chains using graph neural networks. This work demonstrates that GNNs can reveal latent dependencies that are not explicitly documented in enterprise resource planning systems but are essential for accurate demand forecasting and risk analysis. By learning embeddings for nodes based on their network neighbourhood, the authors were able to predict disruption risks and demand fluctuations across many tiers of a supply chain. A key finding of this research is that GNNs outperform traditional link-prediction models by considering the global topology of the supply chain network. This supports our decision to use GAT layers to capture the complex inter-commodity relations in the USGS and SupplyGraph datasets, which are often richer than their taxonomic classifications suggest.

Wu et al. (2019) [13] introduced Graph WaveNet, a deep learning architecture designed for spatio-temporal graph modelling. Graph WaveNet combines a novel graph convolution layer with dilated inception temporal convolutions to effectively capture both spatial and temporal dependencies. A primary feature of this model is the introduction of an adaptive adjacency matrix, which can be learned from data through an end-to-end training process without prior structural knowledge. This approach allowed the model to outperform several established baselines in traffic forecasting. While Graph WaveNet is powerful, its fusion of temporal and structural signals is handled at the feature level internally, rather than through an explicit, interpretable scalar weight. Our project refines this by providing the researcher with a direct, interpretable alpha weight to diagnose the relative importance of these modalities.

Wen et al. (2017) [14] proposed a multi-horizon quantile recurrent forecaster, focusing on the problem of uncertainty quantification in time series. The study demonstrates that providing a single point forecast is often insufficient for decision-making in industrial contexts, where knowing the potential distribution of demand is critical for inventory and procurement management. By using a series of quantile heads to predict different percentiles of the demand distribution, the authors were able to generate calibrated prediction intervals. This research provides the technical foundation for the P10, P50, and P90 heads in our TFT branch, which enable our ATSF model to produce the uncertainty ranges necessary for the downstream risk-scoring module in Layer 5.

Gneiting and Raftery (2007) [15] provided a rigorous mathematical framework for probability forecasting through the concept of strictly proper scoring rules. The authors establish that proper scoring rules, such as the pinball loss and logarithmic score, are essential for evaluating the calibration and sharpness of probabilistic forecasts. A "strictly proper" rule ensures that the model is incentivized to report its true predictive distribution, rather than simply minimizing an error metric that could lead to over- or under-confidence. This theoretical groundwork justifies our use of the multi-quantile pinball loss function in training our ATSF model, ensuring that our prediction intervals are statistically valid and representative of the underlying demand uncertainty.

Lundberg and Lee (2017) [16] introduced SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), a unified framework for interpreting the predictions of complex machine learning models. By assigning an importance value to each feature for a specific prediction, the framework allows researchers to understand the exact contribution of each input variable. This level of post-hoc interpretability is especially relevant in critical mineral forecasting, where a sudden demand spike must be traceable to specific supply-chain or temporal signals. While SHAP is highly versatile, it requires additional computation stages and can become slow for large-scale graph data. This project complements such interpretability methods by providing the in-model, interpretable fusion weight alpha, which quantifies the model's high-level reliance on relational vs. temporal data directly during inference.

International Energy Agency (2024) [17] published the Global Critical Minerals Outlook, underlining the urgent need for advanced demand forecasting in the context of the global transition to clean energy technologies. The report emphasizes that the security of supply for minerals like lithium, cobalt, and copper is essential for the rapid deployment of electric vehicles and renewable energy storage. A key finding is that current forecasting methodologies often rely on static or historical-only data, failing to account for the complex, global inter-dependencies between different mining sectors and geopolitical regimes. This work serves as the socio-economic motivation for our project's focus on USGS mineral data, underscoring the strategic importancia of structural modelling in global resource security.

Wasi et al. (2024) [18] conducted a comprehensive review of graph neural networks in supply chain analytics and optimization, highlighting the concepts, perspectives, and existing benchmarks in the domain. The authors argue that supply chains are naturally graph-structured, and GNNs are uniquely positioned to capture the multi-tier dependencies that traditional tabular models ignore. The review emphasizes the importance of utilizing multi-relational graphs to capture different types of industrial connections, such as co-production or substitutability. This academic context provides the rationale for the 15 typed edges in our USGS dataset, which we leverage via our GAT branch to uncover deeper structural signals that simple categorization cannot reveal.

Wasi et al. (2024) [19] introduced SupplyGraph, a novel benchmark dataset for supply chain planning specifically designed for testing graph-based modelling approaches. The dataset provides daily operational demand for various product groups and includes a variety of edge types representing taxonomic and organizational structures. By providing a standardized benchmark, the authors facilitate the objective comparison of different spatial-temporal modelling architectures. This project uses SupplyGraph as a key benchmark to evaluate our ATSF model in a high-frequency, temporal-dominant regime, where we demonstrate that the adaptive fusion mechanism correctly identifies and adapts to the dataset's structural shallowness (converged alpha approx 0.919).

U.S. Geological Survey (2024) [20] published the Mineral Commodity Summaries, providing the most up-to-date and authoritative annual data on the global production, import, and export of mineral resources. This data source is essential for monitoring global mineral supply chains and assessing geopolitical dependencies. The MCS 2024 summary provides the raw annual observations that underpin the USGS branch of our project. A primary challenge noted in the use of such annual data is the sparse temporal lookback window, where each node often has very few historical observations. Our project addresses this challenge by integrating the rich structural metadata from the USGS reports into a graph framework, allowing the model to learn from inter-node relationships when temporal data is limited.

3.1 Development Methodology

The development of the ATSF model followed an Agile iterative methodology, 
allowing for continuous refinement across the project's distinct phases. As 
illustrated in Figure 3.2, the pipeline is cyclic rather than linear, ensuring 
that insights from the evaluation phase inform subsequent model design 
adjustments. The project was divided into eight critical phases:

1.  **Requirements and Problem Definition**: Identifying the limitations of 
    fixed-weight fusion in multi-modal demand forecasting.
2.  **Dataset Acquisition and Preprocessing**: Cleaning and normalizing the 
    SupplyGraph and USGS Mineral Resources datasets.
3.  **Model Design**: Drafting the five-layer ATSF architecture, including 
    the learnable alpha fusion mechanism.
4.  **Implementation**: Coding the branches, fusion layer, and risk scoring 
    module in PyTorch.
5.  **Training**: Executing multi-seed runs (5 seeds) on GCP Vertex AI to 
    ensure reproducibility.
6.  **Baseline Comparison and Ablation**: Evaluating performance against 
    ARIMA, XGBoost, and isolated branch variants.
7.  **Results Analysis**: Visualizing the converged alpha trajectories and 
    structural attention weights.
8.  **Reporting**: Documenting the findings in the IEEE research paper and 
    this project report.

3.2 System Architecture Overview

The Adaptive Temporal-Structural Fusion (ATSF) model is designed to handle 
multi-modal signals by integrating temporal dynamics and relational dependencies 
within a unified, context-aware framework. As shown in Figure 3.1 (the system 
architecture diagram), the model is composed of five distinct layers: 
(1) a Temporal Fusion Transformer branch, (2) a Graph Attention Network branch, 
(3) a learned adaptive fusion layer, (4) a demand output head, and (5) a 
rule-based risk-scoring interface. The primary objective of this architecture 
is to replace the arbitrary assumption of fixed modality importance with a 
data-driven, per-instance scalar diagnostic.

3.3 TFT Branch (Layer 1)

The temporal branch is responsible for processing historical demand sequences 
and static covariates to capture short- and long-range dependencies. It is 
built upon the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) architecture [1]. The 
branch first passes input features through a Variable Selection Network (VSN), 
which applies soft, learned feature gating to select the most significant 
variables. The processed features are then fed into a two-layer LSTM encoder 
with a hidden dimension of 128 for the SupplyGraph dataset and 64 for the USGS 
dataset. Multi-head self-attention, configured with 4 heads, is applied over the 
LSTM hidden states to capture complex temporal interactions. Simultaneously, 
three quantile heads produce calibrated prediction intervals (P10, P50, and P90) 
via a multi-quantile pinball loss function. This branch outputs a temporal 
embedding and probabilistic forecasts.

represented by typed edges—15 distinct types for the USGS dataset (e.g., 
The structural branch encodes inter-commodity relationships through a two-layer, 
multi-relational Graph Attention Network (GAT) [2], implemented in 
src/models/gat_branch.py as the EdgeTypeAwareGAT class.

Each node in the graph carries four core features computed by 
src/data/graph_builder.py: (1) the recent demand average (7-day rolling mean), 
(2) a price-level proxy (current price divided by historical average price), 
(3) production capacity utilization (recent production divided by peak 
production), and (4) normalized lead time (lead_time_days / 30.0, 
clamped to [0,1]).

Edge features are constructed by concatenating the one-hot encoding of the 
edge type IDs with a scalar edge confidence weight in [0.5, 1.0], yielding 
edge feature tensors of dimension 5 for SupplyGraph (4 edge types + 1 weight) 
and 16 for USGS (15 edge types + 1 weight). The edge type embedding table is 
initialized as an identity matrix (nn.init.eye_) to ensure maximum 
separability at the start of training and to preserve the interpretability of 
individual relation types.

The two-layer architecture operates as follows: the first GATConv layer uses 
head concatenation (concat=True) to produce an intermediate representation of 
size hidden_dim multiplied by 4 attention heads. The final GATConv layer 
averages across heads (concat=False), producing the final structural embedding 
of hidden_dim. Between layers, each GATConv output passes through a 
BatchNorm1d layer (note: BatchNorm, not LayerNorm, as node-level batch 
statistics are more stable for graph data without a sequential dimension), 
followed by ELU activation and dropout. The final layer also returns full 
edge attention weights, which are used for downstream edge-type importance 
analysis in Section 7.6.

A lightweight node importance scorer (a single nn.Linear(hidden_dim, 1) 
followed by sigmoid) produces a per-node scalar criticality value used by 
Layer 5 for dependency risk assessment. A final output projection aligns the 
GAT hidden dimension to the shared model hidden dimension before passing to 
the fusion layer.

3.5 Adaptive Fusion Layer (Layer 3)

The core contribution of this project is a context-conditioned scalar fusion 
weight alpha in (0, 1), which is learned end-to-end with the rest of the model 
parameters. This mechanism determines the optimal balance between the temporal 
and structural branches on a per-instance basis. The full implementation is 
in src/models/fusion_layer.py as the AdaptiveFusionLayer class.

The fusion proceeds in four steps:

Step 1 — Temporal Enrichment: The temporal embedding h_t is passed through 
a residual projection block:

    h_t_enriched = (Linear(h_t) -> LayerNorm -> ReLU) + h_t (residual)

The residual connection preserves raw temporal signal whilst the projection 
aligns the temporal representation into a space more comparable to the 
structural embedding before concatenation.

Step 2 — Context Embedding Lookup: Two separate embedding tables look up 
16-dimensional context vectors: one for the material type (up to 8 categories) 
and one for the forecast horizon (up to 4 options). These context embeddings 
encode domain-level prior knowledge about which modality is typically more 
relevant for a given material category or planning horizon.

Step 3 — Alpha Prediction MLP: The enriched temporal embedding, the structural 
embedding, and the two context vectors are concatenated into a single vector 
of dimension 2*hidden_dim + 32. This is fed through a three-stage MLP:

    Layer 1: Linear(2H+32, H) -> ReLU -> Dropout(0.1)
    Layer 2: Linear(H, H/2) -> ReLU
    Layer 3: Linear(H/2, 1)

The raw scalar output is then passed through a temperature-scaled sigmoid:

Equation (1): alpha = sigmoid(MLP([h't, hs, zm, zh]) / tau)

where tau = 2.0 for both datasets. The temperature (tau > 1) flattens the 
sigmoid curve, preventing premature saturation of alpha to 0 or 1 during early 
training when gradients are large and noisy, thereby preserving the gradient 
signal for learning the optimal modality preference.

Step 4 — Weighted Fusion and Projection: The fused representation is:

Equation (2): h-hat = alpha * h_t + (1 - alpha) * h_s

A final post-fusion block (Linear(H, H) -> LayerNorm) projects the fused 
embedding into the output space before passing it to Layer 4.

To prevent alpha from collapsing to a degenerate extreme, a one-sided boundary 
regularization term is added to the training loss:

Equation (3): L_reg = lambda * (E[ReLU(alpha - 0.95)] + E[ReLU(0.05 - alpha)])

This double-hinge penalty applies only when alpha drifts outside the 
informative [0.05, 0.95] range, whilst freely permitting any value within
that range—including a strong unimodal preference such as the alpha=0.919
observed on SupplyGraph. Lambda is 0.1 for SupplyGraph and 0.05 for USGS.

3.6 Output and Risk Layers (Layers 4-5)

Layer 4 — Fused Demand Representation (src/models/fused_representation.py):
A final linear-plus-LayerNorm projection maps the fused h-hat to point 
forecasts across all horizons. The layer also assembles the full forecast 
dictionary {forecast, std, lower, upper} from the P10/P90 quantile bounds 
produced by the TFT branch and passes them to Layer 5.

Layer 5 — Risk Scoring Interface (src/models/risk_decision_layer.py):
This layer translates point forecasts and graph attention weights into 
procurement risk assessments across three dimensions:

1.  Budget Stress: (forecasted_demand x price) / budget_threshold (default:
    100,000). Risk levels: Low (<0.7), Medium (<0.9), High (<1.1), 
    Critical (>=1.1).
2.  Lead-Time Risk: (forecast x lead_time_days / 30) / current_stock. 
    A safety_factor of 1.5 is applied; ratio above 1.5 triggers Critical.
3.  Dependency Criticality: Mean incoming GAT attention weight per node. 
    Nodes with criticality_score > 0.7 are flagged Critical.

A lightweight learned calibrator MLP (Linear(hidden_dim+3, 32) -> ReLU ->
Linear(32, 1) -> Sigmoid) combines the fused embedding with the three raw
risk scores to produce a single overall_risk_score per product. Four action
flags are generated: WARN budget; URGENT expedite supplier; WARN increase
order; and CRITICAL activate backup supplier.

Lead-time proxy values are synthetic: SupplyGraph group-level constants 
sampled from [3, 30] days; USGS fixed at 1.0. This layer is a 
proof-of-concept and has not been validated against real procurement outcomes.

3.7 Training Objective

The ATSF model is trained using a multi-objective combined loss function,
implemented in src/training/loss.py as AdaptiveFusionLoss:

    L_total = L_MSE + 0.3 * L_pinball + L_reg

where L_pinball is the multi-quantile pinball loss for P10/P50/P90 heads:

    L_pinball = mean(max(q * error, (q-1) * error))  for q in {0.1, 0.5, 0.9}

and L_reg is the one-sided boundary regularization from Equation (3).

The model is optimized with the AdamW optimizer (decoupled weight decay [21]),
learning rate 1e-3. Gradient clipping is applied at L2 norm 1.0 before each
optimizer step. The scheduler is ReduceLROnPlateau (factor=0.3, patience=10
epochs), reducing the LR by 70% whenever validation loss stalls. Training
runs for up to 1,000 epochs with early stopping at patience=50 epochs.

LayerNorm is used throughout TFT and fusion branches. BatchNorm1d is used
in the GAT branch between GATConv layers.

5.5 Graph Construction Design

The graph construction process differs significantly between the two datasets, 
reflecting their distinct industrial characteristics. For the USGS dataset, 
the graph is highly heterogeneous with 127 nodes and 15 distinct edge types. 
These edges represent complex industrial relationships such as technology 
clusters, alloy components, and geopolitical supply risks. This rich 
topology provides a strong structural signal that the ATSF model leverages to 
compensate for sparse temporal data.

In contrast, the SupplyGraph dataset features 40 nodes with 4 taxonomic edge 
types: product_group, sub_group, plant, and storage. These edges encode 
organisational classification rather than causal demand dependencies. 
Consequently, the graph structure is "shallower" in terms of forecasting 
utility, leading the model to learn a strong preference for the temporal 
TFT branch (converged alpha approx 0.919). This autonomous diagnosis of 
structural shallowness is a key feature of the ATSF design.

For the USGS dataset, the graph is highly heterogeneous with 127 nodes and 
15 distinct edge types encoding diverse industrial relationships. The complete 
edge type list, taken verbatim from usgs_loader.py (EDGE_TYPE_NAMES dict, 
lines 44-60), is:

  ID 0:  supply_chain_input         - Raw material to downstream product flows
  ID 1:  technology_cluster         - Shared manufacturing technology families
  ID 2:  alloy_components           - Co-constituent elements in industrial alloys
  ID 3:  construction_coproduction  - Co-production in construction applications
  ID 4:  electronics_coproduction   - Co-production in electronics supply chains
  ID 5:  substitution               - Functional material substitutability
  ID 6:  battery_coproduction       - Co-production in battery/energy storage
  ID 7:  critical_mineral_designation - IEA/USGS critical mineral tier assignment
  ID 8:  geopolitical_supply_risk   - Shared geopolitical supply concentration risk
  ID 9:  price_correlation          - Historical price co-movement groupings
  ID 10: recycling_secondary_source - Secondary supply recovery chains
  ID 11: byproduct_coproduction     - Byproduct co-extraction relationships
  ID 12: catalyst_role              - Shared catalyst application roles
  ID 13: refractory_industrial      - Shared refractory/high-temperature applications
  ID 14: functional_coating         - Shared functional coating applications

Based on the edge_type_analysis.py ablation results in Section 7.6, removing 
technology_cluster (ID 1), alloy_components (ID 2), geopolitical_supply_risk (ID 8),
and substitution (ID 5) degrades USGS RMSE (negative delta), identifying them as 
causally important structural signals. Removing supply_chain_input (ID 0), 
recycling_secondary_source (ID 10), and construction_coproduction (ID 3) 
improves RMSE (positive delta), indicating these edges add noise at annual resolution.

All 15 edge types are encoded as one-hot vectors appended with a scalar confidence 
weight from all_edges.csv, producing 16-dimensional edge feature vectors. Edge 
ordering is sorted deterministically by (source_id, target_id) in the loader.

5.6 Interactive Web Platform and Agentic Pipeline

To bridge the usability gap between raw Graph Neural Network tensors and non-technical 
procurement stakeholders, the ATSF project delivers an agentic web platform built 
on Streamlit. The architecture implements a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) 
framework to translate topological data into strategic business intelligence. 

The platform's frontend architecture consists of four primary modules:

1. Dynamic Data Aggregation (Tabs 1 & 2): 
The system autonomously loads and aggregates cross-seed evaluation logs (e.g., 
`multi_seed_metrics.csv` and `classical_baselines.json`) from both the SupplyGraph 
and USGS datasets. Utilizing Plotly Express and Graph Objects, the dashboard 
renders real-time performance distributions, highlighting the mean Alpha convergence 
gap (0.919 vs. 0.489) and charting edge-ablation impacts (differentiating causal 
structural edges like `substitution` from systemic noise).

2. Dual-LLM Procurement Agent (Tab 3):
The platform integrates the `meta/llama-3.1-70b-instruct` large language model 
via the NVIDIA NIM API (operating at a deterministic temperature of 0.2). When a 
user submits a natural-language business objective (e.g., building a factory), 
the pipeline executes a three-step orchestration:
- Entity Extraction: A custom system prompt forces the LLM to parse the scenario 
  into a comma-separated list of required commodities. These entities are actively 
  cross-referenced against the 127 nodes in the native `NodesIndex.csv`.
- External Concept Graph: The LLM generates strategic procurement advice alongside 
  a hallucinated, JSON-structured blueprint graph representing generic domain knowledge.
- ATSF Native Subgraph: To prevent hallucination, a secondary pipeline extracts a 
  deterministic topological trace directly from the empirical `all_edges.csv` matrix. 
  The system isolates exact structural dependencies (e.g., `alloy_components`, 
  `technology_cluster`) involving the extracted nodes. To maintain UI stability, 
  the trace is thresholded to edge_weight >= 0.8 and capped at the 35 strongest links.

3. Agentic Mathematical Translation:
The raw ATSF structural trace generated in Step 3 is injected into a final LLM 
call utilizing a "Data Science Lead" persona. This forces the model to explain 
precisely why the specific graph phenomena (such as high-attention ablation parameters) 
trigger supply chain volatility for the user's specific request. It guarantees that 
end-users receive mathematically grounded, non-technical risk summaries rather than 
generic internet advice.

4. Physics-Based Graph Engine (Tab 4):
To visualize the massive 6,000+ relationships governing the network, the dashboard 
replaces static SVG arrays with a real-time, force-directed PyVis (vis.js) physics 
simulation. Users can filter by the 15 exact ATSF edge hierarchies. The engine 
employs continuous repulsion mechanics (node_distance=180, central_gravity=0.1, 
spring_length=150, damping=0.09) to autonomously arrange complex multi-node 
clusters without visual overlap, enabling interactive drag-and-drop structural exploration.

6.1 Development Environment

The ATSF model was implemented in Python 3.10 on GCP Vertex AI Workbench with 
a single NVIDIA T4 GPU (16 GB GDDR6 VRAM, CUDA 11.8), verified by the actual 
reproducibility report which records pytorch_version = 2.1.0+cu118 and 
numpy_version = 1.24.3. PyTorch [22] provides all neural network primitives; 
PyTorch Geometric [23] provides GATConv and Data graph primitives.

All hyperparameters are managed by a single `experiments/config.yaml` file 
that is read at runtime by `src/utils/config.py`. Zero hyperparameters are 
hardcoded in Python files, ensuring that any configuration change flows 
transparently from the YAML file to every module.

6.2 Data Preprocessing Implementation

The preprocessing pipeline is modularized into dataset-specific loaders:

-   `supplygraph_loader.py`: Loads the five-feature daily CSV streams 
    (Sales Order, Production, Factory Issue, Delivery to Distributor, 
    plus derived operational_mask as 5th feature channel) for 40 products. 
    Applies per-node per-feature Z-score normalization followed by [-5, 5] 
    clipping to handle zero-inflated outliers. Implements a strict 
    70/15/15 chronological split using integer index slicing rather than 
    sklearn's train_test_split to prevent data leakage across time boundaries.
    The DataLoader uses num_workers=0 (single-threaded) to ensure 
    compatibility with PyTorch Geometric graph objects on GCP environments.

-   `usgs_preprocessing.py`: Parses the raw USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries 
    annual text reports into structured tabular data, extracting 12 features 
    per commodity per year. The 12 features as defined in usgs_loader.py are: 
    Production, Consumption, Imports, Exports, Price, Stocks, Supply, 
    Employment, WorldProduction, NetReliance, NetImportReliance, RecyclingRate.

-   `usgs_loader.py`: Loads cleaned USGS data and applies z-score 
    normalization. Implements cross-validation style loading 
    (rolling_window_cv=True, n_cv_folds=2) to maximise the use of the sparse 
    5-year temporal window.

-   `verify_data.py` and `verify_usgs_integrity.py`: Execute QA checks for 
    NaN tensors, shape mismatches, and normalization range violations before 
    any training begins.

6.3 Model Implementation

The five-layer end-to-end pipeline is assembled in `complete_model.py` as 
the `AdaptiveFusionForecaster` class. The forward pass executes as follows:

1. TFT Branch processes [batch, seq_len, n_features] tensors to produce 
   temporal_embedding [batch, H] and quantile_predictions [batch, horizons, 3].
2. GAT Branch processes the full graph (all nodes at once) via GATConv on 
   edge_index COO format tensors to produce structural_embedding [n_nodes, H].
3. A node-to-batch mapping step uses product_id indices to slice the 
   structural embedding: h_structural = structural_all[product_ids.long()], 
   correctly assembling the per-instance structural context.
4. Adaptive Fusion Layer (Layer 3) combines h_temporal and h_structural 
   via the Alpha MLP, returning fused [batch, H] and alpha [batch, 1].
5. Fused Demand Representation (Layer 4) maps fused to point forecasts 
   and assembles the full forecast dictionary.
6. Risk Scoring (Layer 5) is computed on-demand via compute_risk=True flag, 
   leaving it off during training to avoid unnecessary overhead.

The model also supports a `forward_with_fixed_alpha(batch, fixed_alpha)` 
method, which bypasses the Alpha MLP and forces a user-specified fusion ratio. 
This method is used during ablation evaluation to generate the TFT-only 
(fixed_alpha=1.0), GAT-only (fixed_alpha=0.0), and Fixed Fusion 
(fixed_alpha=0.5) baselines—all using the same model weights and avoiding 
the computational cost of re-training four separate architectures.

6.4 Training Implementation

`trainer.py` implements the `ReproducibleTrainer` class with the following 
key mechanics:

-   At the start of each epoch, a deterministic per-epoch seed is set: 
    `torch.manual_seed(seed_manager.seed + epoch)`. This ensures that 
    dropout patterns and any stochastic augmentation are reproducible 
    without freezing the training randomness entirely.

-   The AdamW optimizer is initialized only after calling 
    `seed_manager.set_seed()`, so weight initialization order is also 
    locked: the SeedManager (src/utils/seed.py) sets all seven randomness 
    sources simultaneously: Python `random`, NumPy, `torch.manual_seed`, 
    `torch.cuda.manual_seed_all`, `cudnn.deterministic=True`, 
    `cudnn.benchmark=False`, `PYTHONHASHSEED`, and `CUBLAS_WORKSPACE_CONFIG`.

-   Per-batch alpha statistics (mean and std) are tracked every epoch and 
    logged to a JSON file: `results/logs/training_history_seed{N}.json`. 
    This provides full transparency into alpha convergence dynamics during 
    training, and is the data source for the alpha trajectory plots 
    (Figure 7.2) and per-category alpha analysis (Table 7.1).

-   Gradient clipping: `nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)` 
    is applied after `loss.backward()` and before `optimizer.step()` on 
    every single batch, preventing LSTM recurrent gradient explosions during 
    Backpropagation Through Time on the 30-day SupplyGraph window.

-   Best-checkpoint saving: Whenever `val_loss < self.best_val_loss`, the 
    full model state_dict, optimizer state_dict, epoch number, and config 
    are saved to `results/checkpoints/best_model_seed{N}.pt`. The convergence
    profile across all five seeds for both datasets is summarised in Table 6.1.

`loss.py` implements the `AdaptiveFusionLoss` class. The exact pinball 
loss computation is:

    errors = y_true - y_pred[..., i]
    loss_i = max(q * errors, (q-1) * errors)

This is the standard pinball (quantile) loss [14][15], which assigns 
asymmetric penalties: for quantile q, underestimation is penalized by 
factor q and overestimation by factor (1-q), incentivizing the network 
to predict the true q-th percentile of the demand distribution.

`metrics.py` implements all evaluation metrics as differentiable PyTorch 
operations:

-   WAPE: sum(|y_true - y_pred|) / (sum(|y_true|) + epsilon) * 100
    Note: WAPE uses a pooled denominator across the batch, making it 
    intrinsically robust to zero-demand entries (unlike MAPE which divides 
    by each individual y_true and explodes for near-zero values).
-   RMSE: sqrt(mean((y_true - y_pred)^2))
-   SMAPE: mean(|y_true - y_pred| / ((|y_true| + |y_pred|)/2 + epsilon)) * 100
-   R2: 1 - SS_res / (SS_tot + epsilon)

`run_multi_seed.py` iterates over the five seeds [42, 123, 456, 789, 1337], 
calling the full training pipeline once per seed and saving individual 
checkpoints and logs for each.

6.5 Baseline and Analysis Implementation

-   `run_classical_baselines.py`: Implements ARIMA [24] (single deterministic 
    run) and XGBoost [25] (single deterministic run) using the same 
    chronological 70/10/20 split applied to the neural models, ensuring 
    fair comparison on identical held-out test sets.

-   `run_graph_baselines.py`: Uses the `forward_with_fixed_alpha` method 
    of AdaptiveFusionForecaster to generate TFT-only (alpha=1.0), 
    GAT-only (alpha=0.0), and Fixed Fusion (alpha=0.5) results from the 
    same trained model weights, guaranteeing ablation consistency.

-   `evaluate.py`: Loads each seed's best checkpoint and runs the model 
    on the held-out test set with `model.eval()` and `torch.no_grad()` 
    to disable dropout and prevent gradient graph construction (which would 
    cause OOM). Assembles P10-P90 coverage statistics from quantile outputs.

-   `edge_type_analysis.py`: Iterates over all USGS edge types. For each 
    type, it constructs a modified graph that excludes edges of that type 
    and re-evaluates the model on the test set, measuring the Delta RMSE 
    relative to the full-graph baseline (RMSE baseline = 0.6317). Negative 
    Delta RMSE indicates the edge type improves performance; positive 
    indicates it introduces noise at the annual temporal resolution.

-   `check_china.py`: A targeted geopolitical stress-test script that masks 
    Chinese production node features and measures resultant changes in USGS 
    node-level GAT attention scores to identify structurally critical 
    producer-commodity relationships.

-   `stats_analysis.py`: Computes the one-tailed Wilcoxon signed-rank test 
    (Wilcoxon, 1945 [26]) across the five seed pairs and calculates the 
    95% confidence interval for USGS WAPE via bootstrap resampling.

6.6 Risk Scoring and Visualisation

-   `demo_risk_scoring.py`: Runs the full ATSF forward pass with 
    `compute_risk=True`, triggering Layer 5 for all test-set nodes. 
    Outputs a JSON file per dataset containing per-product risk assessment 
    dictionaries with keys: product_id, forecast, forecast_std, 
    budget_stress, budget_risk, leadtime_risk_score, leadtime_risk, 
    dependency_criticality, dependency_risk, overall_risk_score, and actions.

-   `visualization.py` and `gen_alpha_fig.py`: Produce publication-grade 
    figures. gen_alpha_fig.py generates the two-panel alpha trajectory 
    plots (Figure 7.2) by reading the per-epoch alpha_mean values from 
    the training_history JSON logs for all five seeds.

-   `gen_edge_attention_fig.py`: Extracts the edge attention weights from 
    the trained GAT branch for the USGS dataset and aggregates mean 
    attention per edge type to generate Figure 7.1.

6.7 Reproducibility and Verification

A 10-second fast-test (`system_dry_run.py`) verifies that the full training 
pipeline initializes correctly without OOM or shape errors before committing 
to overnight full-scale runs.

Final determinism is verified by `verify_reproducibility.py`, which executes 
two independent forward passes under Seed 42 and compares all outputs to

7.1 Experiment Setup

The evaluation of the ATSF model was conducted across five independent random 
seeds (42, 123, 456, 789, and 1337) to ensure the statistical reliability of the 
results. Each seed runs a full training cycle of up to 1,000 epochs with early 
stopping (patience=50) on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU via GCP Vertex AI. The actual 
number of epochs trained per seed varies based on when convergence is detected; 
the best checkpoint model is used for all test-set evaluations. Performance 
metrics for deep learning models are reported as mean +- standard deviation 
across these five runs, whereas classical models (ARIMA and XGBoost) are 
reported as deterministic single-run results. The parameter budgets and 
architectural roles of all six evaluated models are compared in Table 7.0A.

Table 7.0A — Model Complexity Comparison
All neural variants use the identical AdaptiveFusionForecaster model weights
and differ only in the alpha value passed to forward_with_fixed_alpha().
Parameter counts are verified from the reproducibility report (198,000 total).

| Model | Type | Trainable Parameters | Architecture |
|---|---|---|---|
| ARIMA | Classical | 0 (deterministic) | Statistical autoregression |
| XGBoost | Classical | 0 (deterministic) | Gradient-boosted trees |
| TFT-only (alpha=1.0) | Neural (fixed) | ~198,000 | Full ATSF, temporal branch only |
| GAT-only (alpha=0.0) | Neural (fixed) | ~198,000 | Full ATSF, structural branch only |
| Fixed Fusion (alpha=0.5) | Neural (fixed) | ~198,000 | Full ATSF, fixed 50/50 blend |
| **ATSF (Ours, learned alpha)** | Neural (adaptive) | **~198,000** | Full ATSF, adaptive fusion |

All five neural variants share identical parameter counts. The ATSF performance
gain over TFT-only, GAT-only, and Fixed Fusion is therefore not attributable
to additional model capacity. The improvement arises exclusively from the
adaptive routing mechanism of the Alpha MLP, which learns to dynamically
weight the two branches based on the per-instance context (material type and
forecast horizon). This confirms that the ATSF advantage is architectural
rather than parametric.

7.2 Adaptive Fusion Behaviour (alpha Analysis)

The core finding of this project is that the importance of temporal vs. 
structural information is not universal but dataset-dependent. This is 
demonstrated by the converged values of the fusion weight alpha across the two 
datasets, as summarized in Table 7.1.

Table 7.1 — Converged alpha by Dataset and Seed (alpha=1.0: Full TFT; alpha=0.0: Full GAT)

| Dataset | Seed 42 | Seed 123 | Seed 456 | Seed 789 | Seed 1337 | Mean+-Std |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| SupplyGraph | 0.9321 | 0.9182 | 0.9187 | 0.9055 | 0.9219 | 0.919+-0.010 |
| USGS | 0.3400 | 0.6168 | 0.2451 | 0.7248 | 0.5203 | 0.489+-0.197 |

The results show a massive 43.0 percentage-point gap in learned structural 
contribution between the two datasets (8.1% for SupplyGraph vs. 51.1% for USGS). 
On the high-frequency SupplyGraph data, the model converges to near-complete 
temporal dominance (alpha=0.919), reflecting the dataset's shallow structural 
connections. Conversely, on the annual USGS data, alpha oscillates near 0.5, 
reflecting a mixed-regime where the rich structural network of 15 edge types 
provides critical context that compensates for the sparse temporal lookback. 
As illustrated in Figure 7.2, the convergence is consistent across seeds, 
confirming that alpha serves as a robust dataset-level diagnostic.

7.5 Ablation Study

USGS dataset, Fixed Fusion achieves an impressive mean WAPE but exhibits 5x

confirms that a fixed balance fails in ambiguous regimes where the optimal 
modality contribution is context-dependent. Furthermore, on SupplyGraph, the 
temporal-only variant (TFT-only) fails significantly (90.63% WAPE), 
demonstrating that even a shallow graph context is necessary for stabilizing 
model performance in high-frequency environments.

8.1 Privacy Considerations

The ATSF project was developed with a strict adherence to data privacy and 
ethical research standards. No private procurement data, sensitive enterprise 
records, or personally identifiable information (PII) were used during the 
training or evaluation phases. Both datasets utilized in this project—
SupplyGraph [19] and the USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries 2024 [20]—are 
publicly available for research purposes. Furthermore, we explicitly disclose 
that the risk-scoring layer (Layer 5) is a proof-of-concept and should not be 
deployed in real-world scenarios without domain-expert validation to ensure that 
automated decisions do not adversely affect supply chain stakeholders.

8.2 Algorithmic Bias and Fairness

We have identified and documented potential sources of algorithmic bias within 
the evaluated datasets:
-   **Geographic Bias**: The SupplyGraph test partition is predominantly 
    populated by the EEA product group due to the 222-day chronological split 
    window. Users should be aware that performance may differ for other regions.
-   **Data Sparsity**: The USGS dataset operates in a low-data regime (3-4 
    training samples per node). While multi-seed runs mitigate this risk, binary 
    conclusions regarding mineral demand should be interpreted with caution.
-   **Generalisation**: The model's performance has only been validated on two 
    datasets. Its fairness and accuracy in other industrial domains remain to be 
    empirically tested.

8.3 Potential for Misuse

To prevent the potential misuse of the ATSF model, we provide the following 
disclosures:
-   **Risk Layer Limitations**: The risk assessments are based on synthetic proxy 
    lead times. They are intended for demonstration purposes only and must 
    not be used for actual logistics or financial decision-making without 
    incorporating real-world transport logs.
-   **Geopolitical Interpretation**: While the USGS dataset includes a 
    'geopolitical_supply_risk' edge label, the ATSF model is a demand-forecasting 
    system, not a geopolitical prediction tool. The edge labels should be 
    interpreted as relational constraints, not as political forecasts.

8.4 Regulatory Compliance

The project is designed to be consistent with international reporting standards 
for mineral resources. All USGS data is sourced from official publications of 
the United States Geological Survey [20], and the project's sustainability 
framing aligns with the International Energy Agency's (IEA) Critical Minerals 
Outlook 2024 [17]. No proprietary, classified, or restricted-access industrial 
data was used, ensuring full compliance with open-research regulations and 
university ethics policies.

8.5 Sustainability and SDG Impact

As detailed in Section 1.6, the ATSF model contributes to global sustainability 
efforts through its alignment with the UN Sustainable Development Goals:
-   **SDG 9**: By innovating in multi-modal AI, we support the development of 
    resilient and intelligent industrial infrastructure.
-   **SDG 12**: Accurate demand forecasting is a prerequisite for responsible 
    production, as it reduces the material waste associated with over-stocking 
    and systemic forecasting errors.
-   **SDG 13**: By focusing on critical mineral demand, this project supports 
    the long-term planning required for the global transition to a low-carbon 
    economy [17].

9.1 Contributions to the Field

This project successfully developed and evaluated the Adaptive Temporal-Structural 
Fusion (ATSF) model for mineral commodity demand forecasting. Our primary 
contributions are:
1.  **Adaptive Fusion Mechanism**: We implemented a per-instance scalar fusion 
    layer conditioned on material type and forecast horizon, replacing 
    inflexible fixed-weight blending with a context-aware learnable alpha weight.
2.  **Modality Preference Discovery**: We empirically demonstrated that demand 
    signals are dataset-dependent, with alpha converging to 0.919+-0.010 on daily 
    SupplyGraph (temporal dominance) and 0.489+-0.197 on annual USGS data 
    (mixed regime)—a 43-percentage-point structural contribution gap.
3.  **Superior Performance**: ATSF achieved a 30.1% WAPE reduction over the best 
    neural alternative on SupplyGraph and a USGS WAPE of 7.01%+-0.83%, with a 
    95% CI entirely below the strongest classical baseline.
4.  **Operational Readiness**: We integrated a probabilistic uncertainty 
    quantification layer with calibrated P10-P90 intervals and a proof-of-concept 
    risk-scoring interface for procurement planning.
5.  **Interactive Agentic Delivery**: We bridged the usability gap of deep neural 
    forecasting by engineering an LLM-orchestrated Streamlit platform. The system 
    leverages Llama-3.1-70B to dynamically translate empirical PyVis physics graphs 
    and structural ablation metrics into accessible, plain-English procurement insights.

9.2 Limitations and Constraints

Despite its strong performance, the ATSF model is subject to the following 
seven limitations:
1.  **Low-Data Regime**: The USGS dataset contains only five annual observations 
    per node; external validation on longer time series is required.
2.  **Sample Size**: The small sample size (n=5 seeds) limits the achievable 
    Wilcoxon p-value to a minimum of 0.0625.
3.  **Temporal Bias**: The 222-day SupplyGraph window biases the test set 
    predominantly toward late-period EEA product group behavior.
4.  **Synthetic Proxies**: Lead times in Layer 5 are synthetically assigned; 
    the risk interface is not validated against real procurement outcomes.
5.  **Compute Scalability**: Experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU; 
    scalability to larger networks has not been evaluated.
6.  **Static Topology**: The supply-chain graph structure remains static; 
    modelling dynamic or evolving edge weights is a known limitation.
7.  **Generalisation**: The model's cross-domain generalisation beyond the 
    evaluated datasets remains to be broadly validated.

9.3 Future Work Directions

Future research will focus on three primary avenues:
1.  **Dynamic Graph Modelling**: Developing multi-relational graphs with 
    learnable, time-varying edge weights.
2.  **Temporal Extension**: Evaluating the adaptive fusion mechanism on 
    extended USGS time series as additional annual data becomes available.
3.  **Operational Validation**: Conducting prospective validation studies of the 
    risk-scoring layer against real-world procurement and logistics logs.

9.4 Closing Remarks

The ATSF model demonstrates that the balance between temporal dynamics and 
structural relations is a fundamental property of the dataset rather than a 
universal constant. The learnable scalar alpha serves not merely as a tuning 
parameter but as a dataset-level structural diagnostic, quantifying the 
utility of relational information in real-time. By providing both accurate 
forecasts and human-interpretable evidence for those forecasts, ATSF offers a 
robust framework for resilient industrial intelligence.

The `demo_risk_scoring.py` script produces one JSON dictionary per product node.
The complete schema, derived from the RiskScoringLayer.forward() return value 
in `src/models/risk_decision_layer.py`, is as follows:

```json
{
  "product_id": <int>,
  "forecast": <float>,
  "forecast_std": <float>,
  "budget_stress": <float>,
  "budget_risk": "Low" | "Medium" | "High" | "Critical",
  "leadtime_risk_score": <float>,
  "leadtime_risk": "Low" | "Medium" | "High" | "Critical",
  "dependency_criticality": <float>,
  "dependency_risk": "Low" | "Medium" | "High" | "Critical",
  "overall_risk_score": <float in [0,1]>,
  "actions": [<string>, ...]
}
```

Risk level thresholds:
- Budget stress: Low < 0.7 | Medium < 0.9 | High < 1.1 | Critical >= 1.1
- Lead-time risk: Low < 0.5 | Medium < 1.0 | High < 1.5 | Critical >= 1.5
- Dependency criticality: criticality_threshold = 0.7 (from config.yaml)

Action flags generated (rule-based):
- "[WARN] Review budget allocation" (if budget_risk in High/Critical)
- "[URGENT] Expedite supplier" (if leadtime_risk == Critical)
- "[WARN] Consider increasing order quantity" (if leadtime_risk == High)
- "[CRITICAL] Activate backup supplier" (if dependency_risk == Critical)
- "[OK] All risk levels acceptable" (if no flags triggered)
