ADAPTIVE TEMPORAL-STRUCTURAL FUSION MODEL FOR MINERAL COMMODITY DEMAND FORECASTING
________________________________________________________________________________

                              Chapter 7
                         Results and Discussion

This chapter presents a thorough experimental evaluation of the Adaptive
Temporal-Structural Fusion (ATSF) model for mineral commodity demand
forecasting. The evaluation covers the model's learned adaptive fusion behaviour
(alpha convergence), comparative forecasting performance against six baseline
models across two structurally distinct datasets, probabilistic uncertainty
quantification results, ablation study findings, structural attention
interpretability through edge ablation, and statistical consistency analysis
using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test [26]. All performance metrics for deep
learning models are reported as mean +- standard deviation across five
independently seeded runs (seeds 42, 123, 456, 789, and 1337). Classical
baseline models — ARIMA [24] and XGBoost [25] — are single deterministic values.

________________________________________________________________________________
7.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
________________________________________________________________________________

All experiments were executed on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU via GCP Vertex AI
Workbench under PyTorch 2.1.0+cu118. Each of the five random seeds runs a
full training cycle of up to 1,000 epochs with early stopping (patience=50).
The best checkpoint model — selected on minimum validation loss — is used for
all test-set evaluations. As summarised in Table 7.0A, all five neural model
variants share the same ~198,000 trainable parameters of the
AdaptiveFusionForecaster, differing only in the alpha value supplied to the
forward_with_fixed_alpha() method at evaluation time. This design ensures
that any observed performance difference is attributable exclusively to the
fusion strategy rather than to differences in model capacity.

Table 7.0A - Model Complexity Comparison

+---------------------------+-----------------+------------+----------------------------+
| Model                     | Type            | Parameters | Fusion Strategy            |
+---------------------------+-----------------+------------+----------------------------+
| ARIMA                     | Classical       | 0 (det.)   | Statistical autoregression |
| XGBoost                   | Classical       | 0 (det.)   | Gradient-boosted trees     |
| TFT-only (alpha=1.0)      | Neural (fixed)  | ~198,000   | Temporal branch only       |
| GAT-only (alpha=0.0)      | Neural (fixed)  | ~198,000   | Structural branch only     |
| Fixed Fusion (alpha=0.5)  | Neural (fixed)  | ~198,000   | Fixed 50/50 blend          |
| ATSF (Ours, learned alpha)| Neural (adapt.) | ~198,000   | Learned per-instance alpha |
+---------------------------+-----------------+------------+----------------------------+

The identical parameter budget across all five neural variants is a critical
design feature: the ATSF advantage is architectural rather than parametric.
The improvement arises solely from the adaptive routing mechanism of the Alpha
MLP, which dynamically weights the two branches based on per-instance material
type and forecast horizon context.

7.2 ADAPTIVE FUSION BEHAVIOUR (ALPHA ANALYSIS)
________________________________________________________________________________

The core finding of this project is that the importance of temporal versus
structural information is not universal but dataset-dependent. This is
quantified by the converged fusion weight alpha across the two datasets. As
illustrated in Figure 7.2 — which plots the per-epoch alpha trajectory for all
five seeds on both datasets — the convergence behaviour differs strikingly
between SupplyGraph and USGS while remaining consistent across seeds within
each dataset. Table 7.1 records the converged alpha values for each seed.

Table 7.1 - Converged Alpha by Dataset and Seed
(alpha = 1.0: Full TFT; alpha = 0.0: Full GAT)

+-----------+---------+----------+----------+----------+-----------+--------------+
| Dataset   | Seed 42 | Seed 123 | Seed 456 | Seed 789 | Seed 1337 | Mean +- Std  |
+-----------+---------+----------+----------+----------+-----------+--------------+
|SupplyGraph| 0.932   | 0.918    | 0.919    | 0.905    | 0.922     | 0.919+-0.010 |
| USGS      | 0.340   | 0.617    | 0.245    | 0.725    | 0.520     | 0.489+-0.197 |
+-----------+---------+----------+----------+----------+-----------+--------------+

[FIGURE 7.2: Fusion Weight Alpha Evolution Across Five Random Seeds — Two-Panel
Plot: SupplyGraph (top) and USGS (bottom). Per-epoch alpha_mean from
training_history_seed{N}.json logs. Source: gen_alpha_fig.py]

Figure 7.2 - Fusion Weight Alpha Evolution Across Five Random Seeds

On SupplyGraph, alpha consistently converges to near-complete temporal dominance
at 0.919+-0.010 — a negligible seed variance of +-0.010. This reflects the
dataset's shallow structural connections: SupplyGraph's four edge types encode
taxonomic product classification (product_group, sub_group, plant,
storage_location) rather than causal demand dependencies [19]. On the annual
USGS dataset, alpha oscillates near the balanced regime at 0.489+-0.197, with
substantially higher cross-seed variability reflecting the richer structural
differentiation from 15 typed edge relationships and the inherent ambiguity of
the low-data annual regime. This 43.0 percentage-point structural contribution
gap — 8.1% structural contribution on SupplyGraph versus 51.1% on USGS —
constitutes the core empirical finding of the project. The per-category alpha
standard deviation is 0.021 on SupplyGraph (near-deterministic temporal
preference) versus 0.215 on USGS (context-dependent regime), a tenfold
difference. This outcome is a data-driven result of training — not a
hyperparameter artifact — as evidenced by consistent convergence direction
across all five independent seeds.

+---------------+------------------+-------------+------------+----------------+

On the USGS dataset, ATSF achieved a WAPE of 7.01%+-0.83% — a 26.0% reduction
relative to Fixed Fusion (9.47%) and representing the only model whose 95%
confidence interval [5.99%, 8.03%] lies entirely below the deterministic
XGBoost baseline of 8.07%. The R2 of 0.9947+-0.002 accounts for 99.47% of
variance in annual mineral commodity demand. SMAPE of 3.02%+-0.13% is the
tightest across all evaluated models. GAT-only's RMSE std of +-8,388 tonnes
confirms that pure structural reasoning is unstable when temporal observations
are limited to 3-4 training samples per node.

On SupplyGraph, ATSF achieved a WAPE of 55.07%+-1.94%, representing a 30.1%
reduction over the best neural alternative (GAT-only at 78.77%+-5.18%). The
competitive performance of XGBoost (WAPE=52.50%) is expected: a tabular model
that ignores the graph entirely aligns with the dataset's lack of causal
structural signal [19]. As discussed in Section 7.2, this is consistent with
prior findings that graph-based models rely on meaningful relational structure
to provide benefit [12]; the ATSF model provides this structural diagnosis
automatically through its converged alpha value. The higher absolute WAPE values
on SupplyGraph reflect the extreme difficulty of zero-inflated daily operational
demand forecasting on a 222-day window, not a failure of the architecture.
The ATSF RMSE std of +-0.62 kg is the tightest of all neural models evaluated,
demonstrating that adaptive fusion substantially stabilises predictions compared
to all fixed-weight alternatives.

7.4 UNCERTAINTY QUANTIFICATION
________________________________________________________________________________

The ATSF model's probabilistic prediction intervals — generated by the P10, P50,
and P90 quantile heads of the TFT branch — are evaluated using the P10-P90
coverage rate and mean interval width. A well-calibrated model should achieve
coverage near the nominal 80% target. As shown in Table 7.4, both datasets
achieve near-nominal calibration, validating the multi-quantile pinball loss
training objective [14][15].

Table 7.4 - Uncertainty Quantification Results

+------------+-------------------+--------+---------------------+--------------+
| Dataset    | P10-P90 Coverage  | Target | Mean Width          | P50 WAPE     |
+------------+-------------------+--------+---------------------+--------------+
| USGS       | 79.1%+-4.0%       | 80%    | 2,185+-464 tonnes   | 8.30%+-0.88% |
| SupplyGraph| 84.5%+-5.96%      | 80%    | 23.04+-2.18 kg      | 42.54%+-2.97%|
+------------+-------------------+--------+---------------------+--------------+

The USGS dataset achieves 79.1%+-4.0% — within one percentage point of the 80%
nominal target — confirming strong interval calibration. SupplyGraph shows
slight over-coverage at 84.5%+-5.96%, reflecting conservative interval widths
adopted in response to the high volatility and zero-inflation of daily demand.
The mean interval width of 2,185+-464 metric tonnes on USGS represents a
practically actionable uncertainty range for annual procurement planning. The
P50 WAPE of 8.30%+-0.88% — marginally above the point forecast WAPE of 7.01% —
confirms that the median quantile head is consistent with the output head without
systematic directional bias.

7.5 ABLATION STUDY
________________________________________________________________________________

The ablation study highlights the critical necessity of adaptive fusion under
both dataset regimes. On USGS, Fixed Fusion achieves a competitive mean WAPE
of 9.47% but exhibits 5x higher variance (+-5.17%) compared to ATSF (+-0.83%).
A fixed modality balance fails in the ambiguous annual regime where optimal
modality contribution is context-dependent: Fixed Fusion Seed 123 achieved
USGS WAPE of 4.65%, lower than the ATSF mean, but other Fixed Fusion seeds
exceeded 14%, demonstrating that the model satisfies any given planning
requirement only by chance rather than by design. A fixed ratio may approximate
the optimum average but cannot adapt to context, producing inconsistent
performance across seeds [Section 3.8].

On SupplyGraph, TFT-only achieves the worst neural WAPE at 90.63%+-9.06%,
substantially worse than GAT-only at 78.77%+-5.18%, despite alpha converging
strongly toward TFT dominance. This reveals that even shallow structural context
from the GAT branch is necessary to stabilise predictions in high-frequency
zero-inflated environments — when the GAT branch is completely excluded by
forcing alpha=1.0, the fusion layer loses the regularising influence of the
structural embedding. The per-category alpha standard deviation of 0.021 on
SupplyGraph versus 0.215 on USGS confirms that the fusion mechanism is
operating as designed: near-deterministic temporal preference on structured daily
data versus genuine context-sensitivity on sparse annual data.

7.6 STRUCTURAL ATTENTION INTERPRETABILITY
________________________________________________________________________________

The causal utility of the supply-chain graph was validated through systematic
edge ablation on the USGS dataset. Figure 7.1 illustrates the mean structural
attention weights aggregated across all test-set edges for each of the 15 USGS
edge types from the trained GAT branch. As shown in Table 7.5, the full-graph
RMSE baseline is 0.6317; the Delta % column measures the performance change when
each edge type is individually removed.

Removing technology_cluster produces the largest performance degradation (Delta=-2.20%),
confirming it as the most causally important structural signal at annual resolution [Section
4.6]. When this edge type is excluded from the USGS graph, RMSE worsens by 2.20% relative
to the full-graph baseline of 0.6317 — meaning the model loses a uniquely valuable
inter-commodity signal. This finding aligns with Figure 7.1, where technology_cluster
receives the highest mean attention weight from the trained GAT branch, providing
confirmation between the attention-based and ablation-based structural rankings.
Alloy_components (-1.22%), geopolitical_supply_risk (-0.55%), and substitution
(-0.39%) also degrade performance when removed, collectively forming the four edge
types whose causal signals the GAT branch exploits to compensate for the sparse
3-4 annual training observations per node.

Conversely, removing supply_chain_input produces the largest performance improvement
(Delta=+1.29%) — meaning RMSE decreases by 1.29% relative to baseline when this
edge type is excluded — identifying it as noise at annual temporal resolution. Raw
material flow relationships may encode high-frequency production dynamics that become
statistically uninformative when aggregated to an annual grain. Recycling_secondary_
source (+0.50%) and construction_coproduction (+0.39%) similarly improve performance
when removed. The remaining 8 edge types showed Delta% values within +-0.20% of
baseline — effectively neutral at the annual temporal scale of the USGS dataset.
This structured two-group ranking confirms that the GAT branch selectively attends
to meaningful causal connections rather than treating all 15 edge types uniformly.

7.7 STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE
________________________________________________________________________________

The one-tailed Wilcoxon signed-rank test [26] was computed across the five seed
pairs for ATSF against each deep learning baseline. All values in Table 7.6 are
reproduced verbatim from results/Wilcoxon test.txt. We report strong empirical
consistency across seeds as the primary evidence of superiority — NOT "statistical
significance" — acknowledging that n=5 creates a mathematical floor of p=0.0625
that prevents conventional significance thresholds from being satisfied [Limitation 2].

Table 7.6 - Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test Results (H0: no difference; one-tailed)

SupplyGraph Dataset:

+------------------------+----------------+----------------+------------------------+
| Comparison             | WAPE stat / p  | RMSE stat / p  | Interpretation         |
+------------------------+----------------+----------------+------------------------+
| ATSF vs TFT-only       | 0.0 / 0.0625   | 0.0 / 0.0625   | Minimum achievable p   |
| ATSF vs GAT-only       | 0.0 / 0.0625   | 0.0 / 0.0625   | Minimum achievable p   |
| ATSF vs Fixed Fusion   | 0.0 / 0.0625   | 0.0 / 0.0625   | Minimum achievable p   |
+------------------------+----------------+----------------+------------------------+

USGS Dataset:

+------------------------+----------------+----------------+------------------------+
| Comparison             | WAPE stat / p  | RMSE stat / p  | Interpretation         |
+------------------------+----------------+----------------+------------------------+
| ATSF vs TFT-only       | 0.0 / 0.0625   | 0.0 / 0.0625   | Minimum achievable p   |
| ATSF vs GAT-only       | 1.0 / 0.1250   | 1.0 / 0.1250   | NOT significant        |
| ATSF vs Fixed Fusion   | 5.0 / 0.6250   | 6.0 / 0.8125   | NOT significant        |
+------------------------+----------------+----------------+------------------------+

On SupplyGraph, ATSF wins all five seeds against all three neural baselines, 
yielding Wilcoxon statistic=0.0 and p=0.0625 for every comparison. A statistic
of 0.0 means all pairwise differences share the same sign — ATSF outperforms
on every single independently seeded run. On USGS, ATSF wins all five seeds
against TFT-only (p=0.0625). Against GAT-only, p=0.1250, reflecting GAT-only's
high cross-seed variance (WAPE std=6.55%): one GAT seed outperforms the ATSF
mean. Against Fixed Fusion, p=0.6250 reflects that Fixed Fusion Seed 123 achieved
USGS WAPE=4.65%, lower than the ATSF mean, but this exceptional result is
accompanied by seeds exceeding 14%, confirming the fixed-weight model's
unreliability across seeds. The ATSF advantage on USGS is therefore characterised
by lower mean WAPE, 5x lower variance, and a 95% CI entirely below XGBoost.
